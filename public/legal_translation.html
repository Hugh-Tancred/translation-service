Legal translation · European languages to UK English

The legal profession has always needed translators. What has changed is everything else.

Since international litigation began — at the latest in the Middle Ages — lawyers have relied on translators to conduct their business. Even today, national jurisdictions remain jealous guardians of their legal traditions and their languages. The need to respect this is not going away. But the optimal way to meet it has changed profoundly.

The old model

Agencies, intermediaries, and the cost of the middleman

Conventionally, legal translation has been provided through agencies — middleman businesses that assign work to individual freelancers, add their margin, and return the result in days or weeks. For complex legal documents, this was regarded as the only responsible approach: machine translation was considered too blunt an instrument for the conceptual subtlety that legal work demands.

That assessment was correct for its time. It is no longer correct.

What has changed

Twenty years of demonstrating what machines can do

Over the past two decades, the team behind Trans-editor has worked at the intersection of machine translation and legal language — applying AI-assisted translation to submissions, precedents and judgements across some of the largest cases in recent European legal history.

The conclusion from that experience is that the role of human post-editing has been steadily declining — to the point where, for the quality of translation that legal work requires, it is no longer necessary as a matter of course. What is necessary is the right implementation: not a generic translation engine, but a system built specifically for the demands of professional legal language.

What this means in practice

The qualities that legal translation actually requires

"The translation has to be accurate. The terminology has to be right. And the document has to arrive looking like a document — not like text that has been processed and effectively dumped."

Legal translation is not a straightforward linguistic problem. It is a problem of conceptual precision, terminological authority and structural fidelity. A translated contract is only useful if the obligations it describes are rendered with the same clarity as the original. A translated submission or judgement is only useful if the argumentative structure — the relationship between findings, reasoning, and conclusions — survives the translation intact.

This is what Trans-editor is built to deliver: consistency of legal and non-legal wording across the whole document; authority in the choice of English legal terminology; matching of tone and register between source and target; and the structural coherence that makes a complex legal document readable and usable.
